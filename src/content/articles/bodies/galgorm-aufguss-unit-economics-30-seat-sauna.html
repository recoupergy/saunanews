The 2026 UK Aufguss Championships are being held this week at Galgorm Resort near Ballymena in Northern Ireland. The event runs April 20 to 22. Set that aside and the more useful story for US hospitality operators is what the host property has quietly built in the nine years since it added an Aufguss program: a 30-seat sauna that generates roughly £10,000 (about $12,700) in incremental monthly revenue on its own, priced as a £20 (roughly $25) add-on to existing spa packages and stays.

That is the cleanest public unit-economics data point anyone in the European thermal wellness space has put on record. US operators weighing whether to install an Aufguss program, or whether to buy a larger performance sauna rather than a quiet relaxation room, should write the number down.

What Galgorm Actually Built

Galgorm's Celtic Sauna Infusion has been running for nine years, long enough to stop being a novelty and start showing up as a line item. The ritual room seats 30. The resort runs three guided sessions a day on a typical schedule (early afternoon, late afternoon, and evening), each led by an in-house Sauna Master who pours scented water on the stones, choreographs towel work to drive the heated air down onto bathers, and builds a narrative arc across roughly 15 minutes of escalating heat.

The business model is priced the way a boutique fitness studio is priced, not the way a hotel spa is priced. It is a scheduled, bookable, class-style product. Guests already paying for a spa package or overnight stay pay an extra £20 to reserve a seat. The add-on nature is what makes the yield real: the facility, the heater, the energy, and most of the staff cost are already on the P&L.

The Galgorm unit economics: 30-seat sauna, 3 guided sessions per day, £20 add-on per guest, roughly £10,000 (about $12,700) in monthly revenue from a single room. Ritual launched nine years ago. Variable costs are primarily in-house Sauna Master labor and essential oils.

Why the Labor Math Works

The reason Aufguss matters as a format is not the ritual. It is the ratio. Traditional spa services are one-to-one: one therapist, one room, one guest, one hour. Labor in that model routinely consumes the majority of wellness revenue. A single Sauna Master running an Aufguss session is one-to-thirty at Galgorm and one-to-sixty-five at the venue that hosted the 2025 UK finals at ARC in London's Canary Wharf. Some European performance saunas seat 90.

That is the same structural advantage Toronto-based Othership has built its business on. Othership reportedly runs at roughly $140,000 of revenue per employee across 152 staff and $21.3 million of annual revenue, a number that is only possible because its venues are built around 50-to-90-seat performance saunas rather than treatment rooms. The brand raised an $11.3 million Series A last year to expand into the US, including a 14,000-square-foot lease on the Upper East Side. Paris's new contrast club Sant Roch used the same high-density hot-room playbook to clear 85% occupancy in its opening month, and Bathhouse's two New York locations are pacing toward a $120 million run rate on a similar format.

Galgorm is a different business than those standalone urban clubs. It is a resort spa with ordinary guest mix, not a membership-driven bathhouse. That is what makes the £10,000 figure useful. It is what the format yields when it is bolted onto an existing hospitality property rather than being the entire concept.

The Craft Has a Professional Class Now

Galgorm's spa attendant Zak Moore won the 2025 UK Aufguss Championships at ARC in Canary Wharf last year, at age 20. His routine was built around a down-on-his-luck Dublin pub owner who encounters a leprechaun, told in 15 minutes of choreographed towel work and Irish music, with bathers singing along through peak heat around 176°F. He trained for it at Farris Bad, a Norwegian resort, under Lasse Eriksen, who sits on the governing body for the world championships.

The point is not the leprechaun. The point is that Moore is a full-time sauna professional who travels internationally to train under named coaches, competes on a judged circuit, and is now the reference employee for Galgorm's program. That kind of career path did not exist in commercial sauna five years ago. It exists now because the ritual has enough commercial upside to pay for it.

What stood out most was the joy on people's faces.

That was Moore describing what first pulled him from attendant work into the ritual side of the business, in an interview with the Galgorm Resort's own publication after his 2025 win. He will defend his UK title this week in front of an international jury.

The 2026 Circuit Is Real Infrastructure

The Aufguss World Masters, the governing body for the sport, published its 2026 calendar last quarter. It reads like a trade circuit, not a hobby.

  • April 20 to 22, 2026: UK Aufguss Championships, Galgorm Resort, Northern Ireland
  • May 19 to 21, 2026: Aufguss USA Nationals, Bathhouse, New York
  • August 27 to 30, 2026: Aufguss WM Playoffs, Thermen Bussloo, Netherlands
  • September 13 to 20, 2026: Aufguss WM Finals (Show), Satama Sauna Resort, Germany
  • October 1 to 4, 2026: Modern Classic Cup Finals, Farris Bad, Norway

The US leg at Bathhouse's Brooklyn and Flatiron venues in May is the data point most worth tracking. It is the first year Aufguss USA has had an expanded qualification path into the world finals. The 2025 world championships in Verona drew finalists from 18 countries and a crowd of more than 30,000 over six days. The circuit has also bifurcated: "Show Aufguss" keeps the costumes, lighting, and narrative performance, while the new Modern Classic format strips those out and judges practitioners strictly on music, scent, and towel technique. Harvia title-sponsored the 2025 Verona finals using an EOS heater, a move that SaunaNews covered as a deliberate brand-positioning play for the German premium market.

The Science Behind the Show

None of this works without the technical layer. Towel choreography is aerodynamic, not decorative. A bather sitting in a hot room develops a thin film of cooler, saturated air directly against the skin that acts as insulation. The Sauna Master uses the towel to capture the hotter air pooling at the ceiling and drive it downward fast enough to strip that boundary layer off the bather, at which point the humid ambient air condenses on skin and triggers the sudden heat spike that defines the ritual. The various named techniques (the helicopter, the Hungarian eight, the open 360) are all variations on how to move that ceiling air where you want it without injuring the practitioner working in 176°F to 194°F heat for 15 minutes.

Essential oil handling is the other non-negotiable. Sauna stones routinely exceed 392°F. Pouring undiluted essential oil directly onto them is a fire and respiratory hazard, which is why every certification program teaches dilution at roughly 3 to 10 drops per liter of water or crushed ice. The scent architecture is staged: citrus and peppermint top notes at the opening, lavender or floral middle notes as heat escalates, cedarwood or resin base notes for the final round and the walk to the cold plunge. The physiological and social effects of properly run communal heat rituals are starting to show up in published wellbeing research as well.

For US hospitality groups, the takeaway is not that Aufguss is a cultural trend. It is that a performance sauna is a different asset class than a relaxation sauna on the same balance sheet. One is a cost center inside the spa. The other is a bookable, schedulable, pre-priced product with a one-to-thirty labor ratio and a credible incremental revenue line. Galgorm just showed what that line can look like from inside a traditional resort spa, not a standalone urban bathhouse.

Aufguss USA Nationals at Bathhouse in May will be the first clean test of whether the US market has enough trained practitioners to support the format on a national circuit. Watch which US resorts announce in-house Aufguss programs in the second half of 2026, and watch who among the US heater distributors (Finnleo, Amerec, SaunaLife, Almost Heaven) starts marketing performance-sauna configurations with commercial-grade ventilation and tiered seating rather than standard residential builds.

One 30-seat room at Galgorm is generating roughly $12,700 a month as a £20 add-on. That is the proof of concept US resorts and hospitality groups have been waiting for. The next 18 months will separate the operators who treat Aufguss as a real revenue product from the ones who install the room and then leave it quiet.
